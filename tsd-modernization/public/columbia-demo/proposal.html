01Executive summary

The IBC runs on information. Every summer, ~125 newly trained interns become the front line for the safety and experience of nearly 2,700 high-school students — and the handbook says it plainly: authority is having command of information. The challenge is that the information lives in a 60-page handbook, a separate Conduct & Community Standards guide, Canvas decks, GroupMe blasts, Lionmail threads, and a dozen forms — and it has to be recalled correctly, often at 11 PM, by someone three weeks into the job.

We propose to build the IBC Assistant: a private AI assistant trained exclusively on the program's own documents. An RA can ask it anything — in plain language, in any language — and get a direct, accurate answer with a citation to the exact handbook passage it came from. It never invents policy; when something isn't in the materials, it says so and points the RA to their SRA or ibc@columbia.edu. It is live and working today — you can try the working demo here, built on the real Summer 2026 handbook and training rosters you already use.

The short version

Give every intern instant, cited answers to the questions they actually ask on the floor — curfew rules, duty rounds, who to call, what to do when a student is in distress — and give your leadership team a live view of what the staff is unsure about, so training and the handbook keep getting sharper. Lower the load on SRAs and admin; raise the floor on consistency and safety.

02The opportunity

Bishop Switzer — an IBC Resident Assistant this summer — raised this with your directors, who expressed strong interest. Working from inside the program, here is the gap we see, in the program's own terms:

"A key definition of 'authority' in the IBC is that of 'having command of information.' … interns who ask lots of questions in turn have lots of answers." — IBC Residential Life Handbook, Staff Communication & Information

The handbook is excellent and thorough. That thoroughness is exactly why recall is hard under pressure:

  • The information is large and scattered. Residential Life Handbook, the Student Conduct & Community Standards guide, Canvas training decks, GroupMe channels, Lionmail, and forms for curfew, incidents, off-campus passes, POM, and gender-based misconduct.
  • The stakes are high and time-sensitive. A locked-out student at 2 AM, a student in emotional distress, a curfew that differs on weekends, a duty-round schedule, a mandated-reporter obligation — these need the right answer, now, not a best guess.
  • The staff is new every summer. ~125 IBCers and ~1,500 alumni to date; each cohort re-learns the same body of knowledge in a two-week Seminar, then is expected to have it cold for six weeks of Practicum.
  • The load rolls uphill. Every answerable question an RA can't recall becomes a call to an SRA, an RD, or the AD. The handbook's own remedy — "ask lots of questions" — works, but it consumes senior staff time that a well-built assistant can absorb for the routine 80%.
  • Many students and staff are multilingual. The program draws interns and students "from across the USA and world." Answers should be available in the reader's language without a separate translation step.

03The solution — a Retrieval-Augmented assistant

The IBC Assistant uses Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). In plain terms: it does not "make things up" from a general model. For every question it (1) retrieves the most relevant passages from your documents, then (2) composes an answer grounded only in those passages, with a citation back to each source. If the documents don't contain the answer, it declines and escalates rather than guessing.

05Why this fits the IBC specifically

  • It operationalizes your own philosophy. The handbook defines authority as command of information and tells interns to ask lots of questions. The assistant is a tireless place to ask — available the instant a question arises, at 2 AM on a lounge shift.
  • It compresses the learning curve. New interns reach "command of information" faster, and the Seminar's two weeks of policy training get reinforced all summer instead of fading.
  • It protects senior staff time. SRAs, RDs, and the AD keep handling judgment calls and real escalations; the assistant absorbs the high-volume, look-it-up questions.
  • It raises the floor on consistency and safety. Every intern gets the same correct curfew time, the same reporting protocol, the same emergency number — and safety-critical questions always lead with the right call.
  • It makes the handbook a living document. Your analytics show what people actually ask and where they get stuck — feeding next year's handbook and Seminar.

06Data privacy, FERPA & Protection of Minors

This program serves minors and handles sensitive student information, so trust and compliance are first-class requirements — not an afterthought. Our commitments:

  • Your documents stay yours. The corpus is your handbook, policies, and training materials. They are used only to answer your staff's questions and are never sold, shared, or used to train third-party AI models.
  • Built for a minors-serving program. The assistant is scoped to policy and operations. It is explicitly instructed not to give clinical, legal, or Title IX-adjudication advice, and to route all reporting (POM, gender-based misconduct, students of concern) to the documented protocol and the right office — reinforcing your mandated-reporter obligations rather than substituting for them.
  • No student PII required. The base assistant runs entirely on program policy documents. We do not ingest student records, health forms, or rosters of minors unless and until you decide a specific use case warrants it, under your governance.
  • Access control & least privilege. Separate staff, student, and admin scopes; the student-facing assistant sees only a student-appropriate subset of the corpus.
  • FERPA-aligned handling & data residency. We will work within Columbia's data-governance and vendor-security requirements (data handling, retention, access logging, and a written data-processing agreement). The pilot can run on a policy-only corpus that contains no protected student data at all.
  • Transparency by design. Because every answer is cited, your team can always audit why the assistant said what it said.
Low-risk by construction

The Summer 2026 pilot can be built entirely on non-student policy documents — the handbook, conduct guide, and training materials — so it carries essentially no student-data risk while proving the value.
